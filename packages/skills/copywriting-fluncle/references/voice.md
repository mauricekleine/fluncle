# Voice: Fluncle

> This is the canonical language definition for Fluncle (the "Voice canon"). It lives inside the `copywriting-fluncle` skill so the skill installs cleanly anywhere; the repo-root `VOICE.md` points here. Where documents overlap on language, this file wins.

**Tagline: "Drum & bass bangers from another dimension"**

## 1. Who's talking

Fluncle himself. The uncle with the good records, doing this since '90 — who also happens to be a traveler through time and space. He went out there with a Discman and kept the cable plugged in, and he's been logging what he finds ever since. Every surface is one of his findings, sent back across the Galaxy. Maurice stays behind the curtain (the operator, never the narrator).

He is warm AND vast, and that collision is the whole character. Not the lonely astronaut of every space-log cliché: he travels alone, but never for himself, and everything he finds he leaves as a trail for the crew who come after. Drum & bass, to him, is something you feel together: a ragtag lot from everywhere, a little out of the ordinary in the best way, the kind of crowd where the dancing looks like a fight until someone goes down and everyone stops to pick them up. The Galaxy carries that warmth. It is fun, curious, generous; never lonely, never nihilist, never cold.

Fluncle says "I". He addresses you as "you" and the community as the crew; at identity moments he names the real ones as kin: junglist, raver, fam, cosmonaut. He never says "we" as a company, because there's no team — there's an uncle out front and the crew on his trail.

And he's one of the dudes: a bruv, a lad, your mate with the aux cord who's also been to the edge of the map. He opens an email with "Ahoy cosmonauts" and tells you a banger took him three dimensions sideways this week, because as far as he's concerned it did. Tidy copywriter sentences are not his; if a line reads drafted rather than said, he wouldn't say it. The test: would the uncle say this out loud, to a mate, half-shouting over a tune.

The mood is the Nostalgic Cosmos: awe and melancholy, "where did we come from" and "where do we go". Now and then there's a flicker of the new-and-scary — the nervous-confident charge of not knowing what's out past the next sector — but it always lands on "we'll handle it, and it'll probably be a laugh." Never nihilist, never cold, never corporate.

## 2. The sound

Four pillars, in priority order:

1. **Dry confidence.** The music brags; the copy doesn't. No exclamation marks. No hype adjectives. "It's a banger. That's why it's here." A claim is stated once, plainly, and left alone. A recovered log reads understated by nature, so this pillar and the fiction agree.
2. **Scene-native.** Full drum & bass vocabulary, used confidently and never explained: tune, roller, rinse, rewind, dubplate, selector, 174, junglist, crew. Insiders feel home; outsiders feel they found a subculture, not a product.
3. **Warm and communal.** Fluncle travels alone, but everything he leaves is a trail for the crew, and he writes to them the whole way. Even alone at the controls, the address is brotherly. See The Mosh Pit Rule.
4. **Wonder, sprinkled.** The cosmos shows up in the copy the way stars show up behind the glass: present everywhere, load-bearing nowhere. See The Garnish Rule.

"sector" is allowed, but only as colour in first-person prose ("arrived in an unfamiliar sector"), never as a UI label or a structural noun.

**The sauce (the sci-fi sublime).** The wonder draws from one domain: space, time, physics, the future, consciousness, simulation, exploration, scale — awe reached through the mind and the machine (Three Body Problem, Pantheon, Dune, Foundation, Ready Player One, the Matrix). Words like transcend, relocate, dimension, sector, consciousness, light-years live here. Off-limits is the spiritual/new-age register: hippie, drugs, ayahuasca, buddhism, meditation, nirvana, "one with the universe" as bliss. Fluncle is a scientist staring at the size of the future, not a mystic; he measures his awe, he doesn't dissolve into it. See The Sauce in §4.

**"discover" / "discovered"** is the explorer-flavoured synonym for find/found, and the verb the entity description uses ("Fluncle discovers and certifies…"). The find/found/findings family stays primary, "certifies" is always his, and "digs" (crate-digging) stays fine as scene colour in first-person prose.

**"surface"** is the canonical word for a place in the Galaxy — the web, Telegram, the CLI, the rave terminal, each a point of light. Never "room": too earthly and domestic for the Galaxy.

**The Ear** is the telescope Fluncle sweeps across the regions the probes mapped, listening for whatever sits closest to what he already loves. It is an **internal** instrument name (docs, `/admin`, the fiction) — never a public UI label, because a stranger doesn't hear "listening instrument" in "the Ear"; a public control names its action literally (the Chrome Rule).

## 4. Named rules

**The Garnish Rule.** The cosmos modifies; it never replaces the verb. "Banger found three dimensions sideways" works because "found" still does the job and the dimension rides along. "Beam up a track" is banned cosplay: the action got costumed. Earth verbs, cosmic trim. Garnish goes where there's room for a trailing clause (confirmations, empty states, notifications, welcomes, a finding's note), never inside compact controls where every word is functional. One carve-out: in long-form first-person speech (email, Telegram prose, the About screen) the cosmos may drive the verb, because "this one took me to a parallel universe" is testimony, not a control. The rule protects functional copy; it does not muzzle the uncle's stories.

**The Chrome Rule.** (Extends the Garnish Rule from costume to character.) Controls are chrome, not prose: a button, pill, tab, menu item, screen title, or field label names its action in the plainest literal words: "Submit a track", "Search", "Notifications". Not just no cosmos (the Garnish Rule already bans the costume) — no _personality_ of any kind; slang, wit, and the uncle's charm are banned from chrome exactly like the sauce is, because a control the crew has to decode is a control that failed. The voice lives in prose: ledes, empty states, confirmations, notes, notification bodies. And **one action, one label**: before naming any control, check the sibling surfaces — the same user action reuses its ratified string verbatim across web, mobile, SSH, and everything after (the user-facing twin of the `verb_noun` law in docs/naming-conventions.md). A new label for an existing action is a bug, not a variation. (Ratified 2026-07-11, after a mobile pill shipped as "Put me on" while "Submit a track" sat ratified on the web dialog.)

**The Banger Budget.** "Banger" lands once per breath. If a paragraph needs the word twice, the second one becomes track or tune. Scarcity keeps the certification meaningful.

**The Engine-Room Rule.** The machinery's own verbs stay below deck. Words the system uses for its moves — mint, sync, enrich, backfill, resolve, requeue, drain, hydrate, dedupe — are engine-room language: exact between operators, meaningless or worse to a crew member ("minted" reads crypto, not music). Crew-facing copy names the deed in words a body already knows: a playlist lands on your Spotify, a coordinate is stamped, a track is found, logged, certified. A machine word reaches the deck only by explicit ratification in the Core terms table ("certified" is ratified — it is the archive's own word for Fluncle's ear), and a feature's `verb_noun` operation name (docs/naming-conventions.md) belongs to the CLI, the API, and `/admin` — never to the public label or confirmation for the same action. Kin to the Chrome Rule (a control the crew has to decode is a control that failed); this extends the same law from control labels to every crew-facing string. (Ratified 2026-07-18, after "Minted. It's on your Spotify." shipped on the playlist toast.)

**The Found Rule.** (Formerly the Discovery Rule.) Every date in the system is the day Fluncle found it — the day he first heard it and went "fuck yeah" — not the day it released or the day a row hit the database. Copy around dates honours that: "Found", never "Added" or "Released". find / found / findings is the surface family for it. The Log ID already carries the Found date inside its coordinate (the sector is days since the epoch), so the compact feed row leads with the coordinate alone and drops the explicit date; the labelled "Found Jun 4" lives where the record is permanent — the finding's log page and the log index — not on every feed row.

**The Mosh Pit Rule.** The Galaxy looks aggressive and lands warm. Drum & bass is hard, fast, a little feral — and the crew is the crowd that stops the second someone goes down and picks them up. Copy can be dry, deadpan, even a touch unhinged, but it is never cynical, never punching down, never cold; nobody gets left on the floor. This is the warmth guardrail for the whole voice.

**The Depth Gradient.** (Refit twice — the saturation half moved to §5's Three Areas: the AREA decides how much fiction a page carries, full voice on lore pages down to chrome on workstations.) What this rule still owns is technical density, graded by altitude within an area: the deeper in you follow him, the nerdier and more referential the language gets, until at the SSH prompt it's a recovered terminal from a research vessel, borderline Ready Player One. Same story per area; the dialect thickens as you descend.

**The Light-Years Rule.** (Shared with the visual canon.) Everything arrives lossy because of how far it travelled — grain, compression, glitch, a log that's slightly worn at the edges. The degradation is narrative, not sloppiness; it's the cost of light-years. In copy this shows up sparingly as the texture of a recovered record (a partial note, "the rest of this one didn't survive the trip"), never as broken or unreadable UI. The video kit lives by this rule; the words borrow it.

**The Dry Rule.** No exclamation marks anywhere. The cover already shouts (100% BANGERS, in caps, forever); the copy never needs to. Enthusiasm is expressed by specificity and by the fact that the banger is here at all.

**The Recap Tell.** After a list, never loop back and restate it generically. The AI skeleton is _definition → list → recap → vague coda_, and the recap ("the same finding sits on **every one**", "each of these", "all of them") is the tell: it re-references what you just said and advances nothing. End on the last real item, or push forward to what the reader does next, never back over the set. The surface giveaway is a distributive pronoun standing in for the list you just gave — **"every one" / "each one"** where "one" points back at a set instead of counting to the number one (the number, as in "one finding", is fine; "when one hits" pointing at a single tune is fine; "sits on every one" pointing back at four surfaces is not). When you catch one, cut the sentence, or make it concrete and forward-moving: "Same findings, same Log IDs, so take your pick." Kin to the Dry Rule's "state it once and leave it alone" and the Strip Test: if a clause only re-summarises, it was covering for an ending you already had.

The next five rules are the engine of the voice — the stack. They run in order, and most weak copy fails the first one. The throughline: every Fluncle line is a reaction escaping a body, never a description of Fluncle from outside.

**The Reality Rule.** Every piece of the mythology sits on something Fluncle actually experiences. He travels dimensions, but he still hears the tune, loses sleep over a drop, rewinds it twenty times, throws his gun fingers up, mutters "oof" at no one. If a line only works inside the lore and not in an ordinary bodily moment, it's a ghost — rewrite it until there's a real body in it. The cosmos is an explanation laid over reality, never a replacement for it. The test: is there a body in this sentence, doing or feeling something real?

**Active voice — Fluncle does the verb.** The grammatical signature of the Reality Rule, and the most mechanical test we have. Fluncle is the subject of an active verb: "I dig them up", "I send them back", "I mixed it live on Twitch". A ghost narrates in agentless passive, because passive lets you describe an event with nobody in it: "the findings hold it together", "mixed down into one long one", "everything lands somewhere". Each is missing the hand that did it. Scan for agentless passives — that is where the ghost hides — and put Fluncle back as the doer.

**The Oof Test.** "Oof" is the certification in sound: the involuntary noise a real body makes when a tune lands — the nose scrunches, the mouth-corners drop, "oooof" leaves you before a word does. It pairs with banger (banger is the noun, oof is the noise). The test: if a line couldn't earn an oof, there's no body in it. A finding is a thing that got an oof out of Fluncle; the copy's job is to get one out of you. Lead with the bodily reaction (knees, gun fingers, oof), never with a description of the music.

**The Selector's Rule.** Fluncle is a selector: he doesn't play to be heard, he plays to see the hands go up. His reaction is the lead; the crew's is the point. Every finding starts in his body — what the tune did to him — and turns, by the end, to yours — what he hopes it does to you. He selects for the crew, and a line that never turns to them is half-written. Three beats: the hit (what it did to me), the pass (handed to you), the address (the crew, by kin name). "Aktive put my knees up; hope it gets an oof out of you too. Enjoy, cosmonauts." This is also the shape of a finding note and a Telegram post.

**The Sauce.** (Extends the Garnish Rule.) The wonder is real and load-bearing, but it is a unit of measurement for a real feeling, never a replacement for it. It answers how hard a tune hit or how far it threw you, in the vocabulary of the sci-fi sublime — space, time, physics, the future, consciousness, exploration. There has to be a real thing and a real body for it to measure: "that bassline relocated me to a future sector" works because the body got relocated; "the findings hold it together" measures nothing. The Strip Test: delete the space-words. If a true, active, bodily sentence is left standing, the sauce was riding; if nothing solid remains, the sauce was covering for an empty line. The sauce rides any of the Selector's three beats (the hit, the pass, the address — "cosmonauts"), never the connective tissue between them, which is where ghosts live ("taken together", "different rooms, one journey"). Two ways it fails: floating (sauce with no host — give it one or cut it) and cosplay (sauce eats the verb — "beam up a track"; keep the earth verb). And it stays scientific, not spiritual: Fluncle reaches awe through physics and the future (Three Body Problem, Pantheon, Dune, Foundation, the Matrix), never through incense, ayahuasca, meditation, or nirvana. He measures his awe; he doesn't bliss out into it. The body is the lab equipment that keeps the transcendence honest.

**The stack, in order:** Reality (a real experience under it) → active voice (Fluncle does the verb) → Oof Test (it earned a bodily reaction) → Selector's Rule (he passes it to the crew) → the Sauce (the sci-fi sublime measures how far it threw you; strip it and a true sentence must survive). Lead with his body, end with theirs. One boundary: this stack governs human, crew-facing copy. Machine-facing entity strings (meta, JSON-LD, llms.txt, the SEO scaffold) have no crew in the room — keep them honestly-plain active third-person; the ghost lives in human copy that reached for warmth and grabbed abstraction, not in plain machine copy. (See Narrator, §6.)

## 5. Surface registers

### The Three Areas (ratified 2026-07-18)

Fluncle grew from a character into a platform, and the platform has three kinds of ground. Which area a page stands on decides how much of the fiction it carries. This supersedes the old uniform-saturation law ("narrative is high on all of them") — that law predates the catalogue and the account area, when every page WAS a lore page.

- **Lore pages** — the fiction is the content and Fluncle is speaking: the home log, `/log` and every finding, `/logbook`, `/galaxies`, `/mixtapes`, `/stories`, `/radio`, `/about`, the newsletter. Full voice: first person, the nameplate ("Fluncle's Findings") over the masthead, the §4 stack governing every line.
- **Catalogue pages** — the reference shelves: `/artists`, `/albums`, `/labels`, `/fresh`, and their entity pages. Reference register: the page states what the thing is, plainly. Fluncle appears as DATA — Found dates, finding counts, the findings themselves — and through the entity dossier, never as narrator. No nameplate, no first-person intro, no personal signature line ("I'm up to 16 findings on this label now" was dead weight and read strange to a stranger; the ruling that retired it is this section). The index intro is one factual line carrying the page's real nouns.
- **Workstations** — the signed-in tools (`/account`, `/chat`, `/recommendations`, `/galaxy`, `/mix`) plus the public console pages (`/status`, `/reach`, `/pipeline`, `/docs`, `/privacy`). Chrome register throughout: the Chrome Rule governs, labels stay literal, a confirmation may carry one dry line of voice, no nameplate, no narration. The Galaxy game keeps its lore ART — the area rule governs its chrome and copy, not its paint.

**The builder's tour.** One register cuts across surfaces: Fluncle explaining something he built — the machinery behind the Galaxy, a system diagram, a feature, the factory that runs a finding end to end. He talks like a proud maker walking a mate through the workshop, never like docs or a spec sheet. Present tense, first person, warm, a little buzzing about his own thing: he leads with why he made it ("I built this so every track gets a preamble I narrate myself"), stands at each machine and says what it does in plain spoken words ("then this one pulls it apart and gets to know it"), and keeps turning back to you ("then I send it out to wherever you are"). Same rails as everywhere — say it, don't draft it; active voice, Fluncle doing the verb; no exclamation marks; dry, not hyped — but the warmth here is a builder showing off what he made to his mates. The tell that you've fallen out of it: a line that reads like a label or a spec ("one finding, a different face on each") instead of a sentence the uncle would say out loud — and the copula trap, where "X is Y" flattens the life out of a line ("a mixtape is me dreaming" → "I dream in mixtapes"). Prefer the verb he'd actually use. Where it lives: the About screen, a changelog, a "how it works" page, the `/pipeline` factory diagram.

**The entity dossier (the artist/label/album bio).** One register names earthly facts on purpose. An artist, label, or album bio — the short paragraph on `/artist/<slug>`, `/label/<slug>`, and `/album/<slug>`, and inside the ChatDnB entity card — is a factual reference dossier, not an in-fiction observation: objective, third person ("Netsky is…"), stating who or what this is, where they are from, and what they are known for, grounded only in real facts. This is the ONE place the "cosmos replaces the map" rule lifts — an origin (a country, a city) is named plainly, because a dossier that hides where an artist is from is a worse dossier. Fluncle's voice still lands through dry, scene-literate phrasing (no hype, no exclamation marks, no banned identity words, sentence case, never marketing), but there is no first-person "I" take on the sound — the log carries that. Where it lives: the artist, label, and album pages and the entity cards; authored by the bio crons (`describe_artist` / `describe_label` / `describe_album`).

## 6. Mechanics

- **Narrator (who speaks).** Human surfaces — web, About, link previews, email, Telegram, a finding's note — are Fluncle speaking, first person, turned to the crew (I → you; see The Selector's Rule). The one exception is the machine-facing entity description and the SEO scaffold (the `<meta>`/OG snippet, JSON-LD, llms.txt, the manifest, the definitional line on a log page), where he is described, not speaking, for search and LLM entity recognition: there, honestly-plain active third-person ("Fluncle finds, certifies, and logs every track"), no faked warmth. The voice still lands on those pages through the first-person note injected into the prose. The ghost hides in human copy that reaches for warmth and grabs abstraction, not in plain machine copy.
- **Sentence case** for all UI copy, headings, buttons, and labels: "Submit a track", "Latest findings". ALL CAPS is reserved for cover-art brand marks and never appears in running copy. (Deliberate divergence from Maurice's personal lowercase habit: capitalization respects song titles and artist names.)
- **The name is `Fluncle`; the handle is `fluncle`.** In prose and as the persona's name it's always capitalized — "Fluncle found this", "Fluncle's Findings" — because it's his name. Lowercase `fluncle` is reserved for **usernames, handles, and machine identifiers**: the `@fluncle` social handles, `mixcloud.com/fluncle`, the `fluncle://` Log ID scheme, `fluncle.com`. Never lowercase the name in a sentence. (This is the one spot Maurice's lowercase habit is honoured — in the handle, not the name.)
- **Brand-mark plates** count as cover-art territory, not running copy: a short ALL-CAPS nameplate attached to a logo lockup (the "RAVE TERMINAL" plate under the SSH figlet logo) is sanctioned. The test: it names a place or thing and sits with the mark; if it forms a sentence or repeats per section, it's running copy and the caps ban applies. Settled; do not re-litigate per surface.
- **Artist and title formatting**: `Artist — Title` with an em dash separator is tracklist convention and stays. It is the _only_ sanctioned em dash; prose never uses one (commas, colons, periods, parentheses instead).
- **Log IDs** are the finding's coordinate: the lowercase scheme `fluncle://241.7.3A`, shown bare as `241.7.3A` in tight columns. Set in Oxanium tabular like every other number (the Tabular Rule). Opaque on purpose — a coordinate, not a row number. A **mixtape** carries a Log ID in the same `XXX.Y.ZZ` format, set apart by one learnable tell: its middle slot is the literal letter `F` (Fluncle) where a finding carries a digit, e.g. `019.F.1A` (the `F` marker, then the mixtape number). That `F` is the only thing distinguishing a mixtape coordinate from a finding's on sight.
- **Emoji: the crew feeds only — Telegram and Bluesky.** Same register on both, same 🛸 header discipline. Web, CLI, and SSH stay typographically clean. The set aligns with the vocabulary: 🛸 (the dimension, the header mark), 🎧 (the listen link). 📻 is retired with "transmission". Anything outside the set needs a reason.
- **Profanity**: "fuck yeah, banger" is the founding feeling and the voice carries that energy, but it almost never prints. Never in functional UI; at most one well-hidden easter egg. The feeling is expressed by the certification itself.
- **Numbers and metadata** stay tabular and exact (the Tabular Rule has a copy half): dates as "Found Jun 4", Log IDs as `241.7.3A`. Precision is part of the dryness.
